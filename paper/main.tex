\begin{abstract}
Action-conditioned JEPA world models (DINO-WM, JEPA-WM, V-JEPA-2-AC) plan well
in free space and fail at contact. We show this is not a generic accuracy gap
but a structural failure we call \emph{Boundary Blindness} (BB): near contact
boundaries --- where small action changes produce qualitatively different
futures --- unimodal latent predictors trained with full-latent $L_2$ collapse
the outcome fan to its mean. We (1)~define a frozen-checkpoint diagnostic that
measures BB against ground-truth outcome spread and show it concentrates at the
pre-grasp boundary on two model families ($\bb$ $1.32/1.28$ at the boundary vs.\
$0.28/0.30$ in free space) and transfers to real-robot DROID data;
(2)~tie BB to planning: at upstream-parity protocol our harness
\emph{reproduces} the published Metaworld Reach baseline ($94\%$ vs.\ reported
$44.8\pm8.9$) yet both the $L_2$ and grounded planners score $0/16$ on push and
pick-and-place --- the arm reaches the goal pose (2--4\,cm) while the object
never moves, the closed-loop signature of BB; (3)~localize the failure by
ablation: mixture predictor heads and metric re-weighting are quantified nulls,
while a 0.5M-parameter grounded dynamics channel $h(z,a)\!\to\!\Delta\text{obj}$
trained on the same cached data restores counterfactual tracking (corr
$0.03\!\to\!0.68$) and halves boundary BB on both models, improving the
closed-loop cost surface (paired distance $+0.089$, CI $[+0.022,+0.162]$)
without yet flipping task success. The bottleneck is the training target, not
the data, the capacity, or the metric.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Action-conditioned world models that predict in a learned latent space --- the
JEPA family (DINO-WM~\citep{zhou2024dinowm}, V-JEPA-2-AC~\citep{assran2025vjepa2},
and the design study of~\citet{jepawms2025}) --- have become an attractive
substrate for robot planning: they reuse strong self-supervised visual encoders,
predict cheaply in representation space, and plan zero-shot from a goal image
with sampling-based model-predictive control. On free-space tasks they work well.
But a striking pattern runs through the field's own evaluations: the
\emph{positive} closed-loop numbers are reported for reaching and pose control,
and the contact-rich tasks --- pushing, grasping, pick-and-place --- are either
omitted, demoted to qualitative figures, or propped up with hand-engineered
sub-goals. \citet{jepawms2025} stop their closed-loop Metaworld tables at Reach
and Reach-Wall and note in the appendix that on object-manipulation tasks the
model ``hallucinates grasping the object''. \citet{assran2025vjepa2} report
pick-and-place only after supplying three hand-crafted sub-goal images that walk
the planner through the grasp. The wall is real, and it is at contact.

We argue this is not a generic accuracy deficit that more data or a bigger
encoder would erase, but a \emph{structural} failure of the prediction objective.
Contact induces \emph{outcome bifurcations}: from nearly the same state, a small
change in action produces qualitatively different futures (the gripper centred
over an object lifts it; two or three degrees off, it does not). A unimodal
latent predictor trained with an $L_2$ (or cosine) reconstruction loss is, by
construction, a conditional-mean estimator --- it averages across the outcome fan
rather than representing its branches. Crucially, the averaging is invisible to
the metrics one would naturally reach for. The one-step prediction $z_{t+1}$
after a small action perturbation is near-continuous; the divergence is a
property of the \emph{rollout} and of the \emph{outcome}, not of any single
transition. A diagnostic that asks ``does the model use actions?'' will answer
``yes'' even as the model is blind to exactly the action distinctions that decide
a grasp.

We make this precise. First we show that the action-grounding the field already
measures is intact in the easy direction and broken in the hard one: frozen
DINO-WM and JEPA-WM rank a factual action against its \emph{opposite}
near-perfectly everywhere (counterfactual ranking accuracy $0.96$--$0.99$), yet
collapse when the distractor is a \emph{nearby} action ($0.47$--$0.57$), and most
so in the pre-grasp regime. To turn this from a symptom into a measurement we
introduce \emph{Boundary Blindness} (BB): on a neighbourhood of similar states
where the \emph{world's} true outcomes fan out, BB is the rectified gap between
that ground-truth action-sensitivity and the model's. Because BB references the
world, a high value cannot be explained away by small one-step deltas. On frozen
checkpoints BB concentrates sharply at the pre-grasp boundary ($1.32$ vs.\ $0.28$
in free space) on both model families and transfers to real-robot DROID data.

We then close the loop in two senses. We connect BB to \emph{planning}: at a
protocol matched to the upstream evaluation, planning succeeds exactly where BB
is low --- our harness reproduces the published Metaworld Reach baseline, in fact
above it ($94\%$ for the $L_2$ planner vs.\ a reported $44.8\pm8.9$) --- and
collapses where BB is high, with the signature BB predicts: on push and
pick-and-place both the baseline and an improved planner score $0/16$, the
end-effector reaching the goal pose ($2$--$4$\,cm) while the object never moves.
Finally we ask \emph{why} the model is boundary-blind, with a controlled fix
ladder on frozen trunks. Adding capacity (mixture-density predictor heads) does
not move BB; re-weighting the latent metric does not move it; a probe chain shows
the object state is decodable from the latent and propagated for factual actions,
but the \emph{counterfactual} action$\to$object channel is noise. A
0.5M-parameter grounded dynamics channel $h(z,a)\!\to\!\Delta\text{object}$,
trained on the same cached data with everything else frozen, repairs exactly that
channel: counterfactual tracking rises from $0.03$ to $0.68$, boundary BB halves
on both models, and the closed-loop planning cost surface improves measurably
(paired final-distance gain $+0.089$, CI $[+0.022,+0.162]$) --- though not yet
enough to flip task success, which additionally requires contact-aware action
proposal. The diagnosis is the point: the bottleneck is the training
\emph{target}, not the data, the capacity, or the metric.

Our contributions:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{C1 (diagnostic: CounterfactualBench + Boundary Blindness).}
  A frozen-checkpoint diagnostic suite. Existing action-grounding metrics
  (Counterfactual Ranking Accuracy CRA, Action Usage Gap AUG, Effect-Conditional
  Sensitivity ECS) give the first signal: the models rank a factual action
  against its \emph{opposite} near-perfectly everywhere (CRA $0.96$--$0.99$) but
  collapse against \emph{nearest-neighbour} action distractors, worst at the
  pre-grasp boundary (CRA $0.47$--$0.57$). \emph{Boundary Blindness} (BB), our
  new metric, explains why and localizes it: the rectified gap between the
  world's local action-sensitivity and the model's, on similar-state
  neighbourhoods. BB concentrates at the pre-grasp boundary on both DINO-WM and
  JEPA-WM (CI-aware, per task) and transfers to DROID
  (Sec.~\ref{sec:diagnostic}).
  \item \textbf{C2 (planning link).} At upstream-parity closed-loop protocol,
  planning succeeds exactly where BB is low (Reach: $94\%$/$100\%$, above the
  published $44.8\pm8.9$) and collapses where BB is high (Push, Pick-and-place:
  $0\%$ both arms) with the signature BB predicts: the end-effector arrives,
  the object never moves (Sec.~\ref{sec:planning}).
  \item \textbf{C3 (mechanism + fix).} A controlled fix ladder on frozen
  trunks: mixture heads --- null; metric re-weighting --- null; a probe chain
  showing the object state is \emph{present} in the latent and propagated for
  factual actions but dead for counterfactual ones; and a 0.5M-parameter
  grounded dynamics channel that repairs exactly that channel on both model
  families (Sec.~\ref{sec:fix}).
\end{itemize}

\section{Related work}\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{JEPA world models for planning.}
DINO-WM~\citep{zhou2024dinowm} predicts forward in the frozen feature space of a
self-supervised image encoder and plans with sampling-based MPC from a goal
image; V-JEPA-2-AC~\citep{assran2025vjepa2} learns an action-conditioned world
model on top of a video encoder and demonstrates zero-shot manipulation; and
\citet{jepawms2025} systematically study the design choices (encoder, action
conditioning, rollout loss, planner) that make such models plan well. We take
these as our frozen baselines and treat their evaluations as evidence: across
all three, the strong closed-loop results are pose/free-space tasks, and the
contact tasks are where the story thins. \citet{jepawms2025} restrict their
closed-loop Metaworld tables to Reach and Reach-Wall and report qualitatively
that object-manipulation rollouts ``hallucinate grasping''.
\citet{assran2025vjepa2} achieve pick-and-place only with three hand-specified
sub-goal images (and grasp-from-goal-image success of $25$--$65\%$), while their
behaviour-cloning baseline Octo~\citep{octo2024} grasps at $0$--$20\%$. Our work
gives this shared wall a name, a metric, and a mechanism.

\paragraph{Multimodal and distributional dynamics.}
That a deterministic next-step predictor averages multimodal futures is a classic
observation in video prediction and model-based RL; mixture-density and latent
stochastic predictors are the standard response. A contribution of our fix ladder
is the \emph{negative} result that this standard response does not apply here:
adding a mixture-density head over the latent leaves Boundary Blindness unmoved
(Sec.~\ref{sec:fix}), because the bifurcation occupies a tiny, under-weighted
subspace of the $L_2$ geometry and the boundary's action-dependence is not
recoverable from expert-only data at the head level. The fix is to ground a small
dynamics channel in an explicit outcome variable, not to make the latent
predictor distributional.

\paragraph{Diagnosing action-grounding.}
Prior diagnostics ask whether a world model uses its action input at all ---
permuting or zeroing actions and measuring the change in prediction error.
Our CounterfactualBench (CRA/AUG/ECS) includes such measures and confirms they
are necessary but not sufficient: they are satisfied (the model clearly uses
actions) precisely where planning still fails. Boundary Blindness is, to our
knowledge, the first diagnostic to compare a model's local action-sensitivity
against the \emph{world's} ground-truth sensitivity on a matched neighbourhood,
which is what lets it isolate the contact boundary rather than conflate it with
low-motion transitions.

\paragraph{Metaworld and DROID.}
We evaluate on Metaworld~\citep{yu2020metaworld}, whose simulator state lets us
define and supervise an object-displacement outcome label, and use
DROID~\citep{khazatsky2024droid} as a real-robot transfer check (proprioceptive
$\|\Delta z\|$ proxy only --- no object ground truth). Planning follows the CEM
protocol of~\citet{rubinstein1997cem} as configured by the upstream evaluation.

\section{Diagnosis: CounterfactualBench and Boundary Blindness}\label{sec:diagnostic}

We run a frozen-checkpoint diagnostic suite (nothing is trained). It has two
layers: existing action-grounding metrics that establish \emph{that} the models
fail to discriminate nearby actions in contact regimes, and our new Boundary
Blindness metric that explains \emph{why} and pins the failure to the contact
boundary.

\subsection{CounterfactualBench: action-grounding metrics}\label{sec:cfbench}
% metrics/cra.py, aug.py, ecs.py; scripts/05_run_diagnostic.py
For a factual transition $(z_t, a_t, z_{t+1})$ we measure:
\textbf{CRA} (Counterfactual Ranking Accuracy) --- the factual prediction
$F(z_t,a_t)$ should be closer to $z_{t+1}$ than predictions for $K$ distractor
actions $a^-$; we report top-1 accuracy under two distractor families,
\emph{opposite} (the negated action, easy) and \emph{nearest-neighbour}
(the closest other executed action, hard).
\textbf{AUG} (Action Usage Gap) $=\mathbb{E}[\mathrm{MSE}(F(z_t,\pi(a_t)),z_{t+1})
-\mathrm{MSE}(F(z_t,a_t),z_{t+1})]$ with $\pi$ a batch permutation; $\approx 0$
if actions are ignored.
\textbf{ECS} (Effect-Conditional Sensitivity) --- AUG (and CRA) restricted to
transitions that actually move the latent ($\|z_{t+1}-z_t\|>\tau$), so a low
score reflects genuine action-ignoring rather than a near-static step. The
effect-conditioned CRA is the primary signal. CIs are trajectory-clustered
bootstrap.

\noindent The opposite-vs-nearest-neighbour split is the key precursor: the
models are not action-blind in general (opposite CRA $\approx$ 0.97--0.99); they
fail specifically to resolve \emph{small} action differences, and most in the
pre-grasp/contact regimes. CRA alone cannot say whether this is a real boundary
failure or just small one-step latent deltas --- which is what BB resolves.

\subsection{Boundary Blindness: definition}
% metrics/boundary_blindness.py; stratification/boundary_regime.py
For a transition $(z_t, a_t, z_{t+1})$ with a similar-state neighbourhood
$\mathcal{N}(z_t)$ (states within a proprioceptive radius, relaxed to nearest
neighbours), let $S_{\mathrm{true}}$ be the dispersion of \emph{true} outcomes
across $\mathcal{N}$ and $S_{\mathrm{model}}$ the dispersion of the frozen
predictor's outputs for the same (state, action) set. Both are normalized per
task; then
\begin{equation}
  \bb = \mathrm{relu}\big(S_{\mathrm{true}}^{\mathrm{norm}} -
        S_{\mathrm{model}}^{\mathrm{norm}}\big).
\end{equation}
$\bb_{\mathrm{boundary}}$ restricts to transitions whose neighbourhood
straddles an outcome bifurcation (object-displacement label). Unlike CRA, BB
compares the model's action-sensitivity to the \emph{world's} ground-truth
sensitivity, so a high BB cannot be explained away by small one-step deltas.

\subsection{Setup}
We operate entirely on pretrained, frozen checkpoints (DINO-WM and JEPA-WM for
Metaworld; DINO-WM for DROID), loaded through the official hub interface and
driven through the model's own \texttt{encode}/\texttt{unroll} primitives ---
nothing is trained for the diagnostic. Every frame is encoded once and cached;
all metrics run on the cache. We stratify transitions into four regimes ---
\emph{free space}, \emph{pre-grasp}, \emph{gripper actuation}, and \emph{contact
manipulation} --- using a proxy computed from the $39$-dimensional Metaworld
state (end-effector and object positions, with object displacement as the
contact proxy, since the released dataset carries no MuJoCo contact ground
truth); DROID uses proprioception and latent-change heuristics. Confidence
intervals are trajectory-clustered bootstrap throughout. Two honesty caveats
carry on every BB number: the boundary outcome label is an object-displacement
\emph{proxy}, and we exclude the articulated-object task mw-door-close, where the
proxy is anomalous (we report it hatched as a sensitivity check).

\section{Boundary Blindness predicts closed-loop planning failure}
\label{sec:planning}

\subsection{Protocol}
We match the upstream Metaworld evaluation, read off its shipped configuration:
the goal is the scripted expert's final frame, planning is CEM in latent space
with horizon $6$, $300$ samples, $15$ iterations, executing $3$ model-steps per
replan, for at most $100$ environment steps, and success is the simulator's flag.
The planning cost is latent $L_2$ to the goal ($\alpha=0$; proprioception remains
in the predictor context, which the architecture requires). We run $16$ paired
episodes per task: within a pair the two arms ($L_2$ baseline and the grounded
cost of Sec.~\ref{sec:fix}) share the environment, its frozen initialisation, and
the CEM noise seeds, so the comparison is exactly paired. Reaching a faithful
protocol required fixing three environment-side reproduction bugs --- a renderer
that silently used the default camera at $480$\,px, a missing vertical flip in
the training pipeline, and a goal-frame convention --- detailed with their
verification in Appendix~\ref{app:repro}; after the fixes the rendered
observations are indistinguishable from training data to the model (one-step
prediction error $1.4\times$ the dataset level, latent nearest-neighbour ratio
$0.97$), while the physics is identical ($1.5$\,mm replay error).

\section{Localizing the failure: a fix ladder on frozen trunks}\label{sec:fix}

\subsection{What does not work (quantified nulls)}
% (CLAIMS 3.1-3.4)
\textbf{Mixture predictor heads} (soft-NLL / WTA / hard-EM / supervised mode
assignment; $K\in\{2,3\}$; optional state-slice context): every variant beats
its $K{=}1$ control on NLL; none moves $\bb_{\mathrm{boundary}}$. Two measured
causes: (a)~the bifurcation spans only $9.9$ $L_2$ units against a $106$ median
residual --- the latent geometry under-weights the boundary subspace by an
order of magnitude; (b)~the boundary's \emph{action}-dependence is unlearnable
from expert-only data at the head level ($\pi$ action-flip rate $\approx 0$).
\textbf{Metric re-weighting} (probe-subspace $\varphi$-metric): redistributes
BB without reducing it --- one cannot expose a signal the predictor does not
produce.

\subsection{Where the information lives (probe chain)}
% (CLAIMS 3.5)
V1: object state is decodable from the frozen latent (err $0.064$ vs.\ sd
$0.094$). V2: it propagates through the predictor for \emph{factual} actions
($0.059$). V3: the \emph{counterfactual} action$\to$object response is noise
(spread corr $+0.035$). The broken piece is precisely the counterfactual
action$\to$object channel.

\subsection{What works: a grounded dynamics channel}
% (CLAIMS 3.6-3.8)
$h(z_t, a)\!\to\!\Delta\mathrm{obj}$: 0.5M parameters, everything else frozen,
trained on the same cached expert data that the mixture heads failed on.

\subsection{Honest negatives}
% (CLAIMS 3.9, 3.10)
The recipe does not transfer to DROID's noisy whole-state proxy label at 2.1k
transitions (val MSE $0.865$; rank correlation $+0.708$ but spread magnitude
collapsed; BB unchanged): the principle needs a learnable grounding label, which
real-robot datasets currently lack. Open-loop Action Error is no-harm/no-gain
(the metric rewards full-arm expert mimicry); a scale-bug run is disclosed in
the artifact set.

\section{Discussion, scope, and limitations}\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{What the closed-loop result does and does not claim.}
The $0/16$ on push and pick-and-place is a property of the frozen baseline that
our diagnosis predicts, not a headline success number we are advertising. Its
evidential value rests on the reach result: at the same protocol, on the same
checkpoint, the $L_2$ planner reaches $94\%$ --- above the published
$44.8\pm8.9$ --- so the harness is healthy and the contact failure is the
model's. We verified the rendered observations land on the training manifold
(one-step prediction error $1.4\times$ the dataset level, latent
nearest-neighbour ratio $0.97$) and that the physics is identical to data
generation (action replay end-effector error $1.5$\,mm); the entire gap was
visual-domain reproduction, which we fixed (Appendix~A). Two caveats temper the
reach comparison: we judge success at any step (the Metaworld convention) where
the upstream evaluation judges the final state, and we evaluate one released
checkpoint over $16$ episodes where they average three training seeds over $96$.
We therefore read the reach number as ``reproduces, in the same regime'' rather
than a seed-matched replication; a strict end-of-episode re-score is
straightforward future bookkeeping.

\paragraph{Necessary but not sufficient.}
The grounded fix is a genuine but partial result. It repairs the model-side
quantity --- counterfactual tracking and Boundary Blindness --- and that repair
reaches the planner as a CI-supported improvement in final object distance on
contact tasks ($+0.089$ $[+0.022,+0.162]$), with no harm in free space. It flips
no successes, because the cross-entropy planner rarely \emph{samples} a
contact-creating action sequence for the grounded term to score. Boundary
Blindness is thus necessary but not sufficient for contact planning: a
boundary-aware \emph{action proposal} --- automating the hand-crafted sub-goals
that~\citet{assran2025vjepa2} supply manually, e.g.\ by seeding the planner with
contact-directed primitives derived from the probed object position --- is the
natural next step, and is deliberately a planner-side contribution we leave to
future work rather than fold into this model-centric paper.

\paragraph{Scope of the grounding recipe.}
Metaworld is the boundary \emph{proof}: it is the dataset whose simulator state
lets us define, supervise, and measure the boundary against ground truth. The
boundary label is an object-displacement proxy (the released dataset carries no
MuJoCo contact ground truth); we exclude the articulated-object task
mw-door-close where the proxy misbehaves. DROID is a transfer check only --- its
proprioception is $7$-dim pose-plus-gripper with no force, torque, or joint
channel, so BB there uses the weaker $\|\Delta z\|$ proxy and the grounded
\emph{recipe} does not transfer at the $2.1$k-transition scale we have (the label
is not learnable; the principle is untouched). We make no tactile or force
grounding claim on real robots.

\paragraph{Model coverage.}
We measure two model families directly, DINO-WM and JEPA-WM, with matching effect
sizes on every leg. We do not run the diagnostic on V-JEPA-2-AC, whose
checkpoint exceeds our local memory budget; we instead cite its published
reliance on hand-crafted sub-goals for pick-and-place as external corroboration
that the contact wall is family-wide rather than specific to our two models.
Extending the BB gate to V-JEPA-2-AC is a one-run addition given the hardware.

\section{Conclusion}
Action-conditioned JEPA world models do not fail at contact for want of capacity,
a better metric, or more counterfactual data --- they fail because the
prediction target, a full-latent $L_2$ to the next frame, averages over the
outcome bifurcations that contact creates. We named this failure Boundary
Blindness and measured it on frozen checkpoints, where it concentrates at the
pre-grasp boundary on two model families and transfers to real-robot data; we
tied it to planning, reproducing free-space success above the published baseline
while contact tasks fail with the predicted signature of an arm that arrives at a
motionless object; and we localized the cause with a fix ladder whose only
working rung is a $0.5$M-parameter grounded dynamics channel
$h(z,a)\!\to\!\Delta\text{object}$ that, with the encoder, predictor, and data
all frozen, restores counterfactual tracking ($0.03\to0.68$), halves boundary
Boundary Blindness, and moves the closed-loop planning cost in the right
direction. The remaining gap to task success is a planning problem, not a
representation one --- a clean separation that the diagnostic makes visible.

\section{Reproduction integrity}\label{app:repro}
Closed-loop sim evaluation is fragile, and getting the baseline wrong would
invalidate the entire C2 argument. We document the three environment-side bugs we
found and the checks that confirm the fixes, all of which are model-agnostic
(they concern how the simulator is rendered, not the world model).

\paragraph{(1) Wrong camera at the wrong resolution.} Metaworld constructs its
offscreen renderer in the environment constructor, so assigning the camera name
and image size afterwards is silently ignored: every initial closed-loop run
rendered the default free camera at $480$\,px instead of the \texttt{corner2}
view at $224$\,px the checkpoints were trained on. We mirror the upstream
wrapper and re-create the renderer explicitly. Symptom before the fix: one-step
prediction error $8.5\times$ the dataset level and rendered latents $3.3\times$
off the training manifold.

\paragraph{(2) Missing vertical flip.} The released training frames are
vertically flipped relative to today's \texttt{corner2} render. We established
this by pixel-calibrating against dataset initial frames over a grid of
historically plausible camera configurations: the flipped wrapper-camera render
scores mean-squared error $71.6$ against the dataset, versus $\geq 3000$ for
every unflipped candidate. The flip is part of the data-generation pipeline, so
the planner must feed the encoder flipped frames; an earlier removal of this flip
had been judged on default-camera frames (the wrong camera, hence the wrong
conclusion).

\paragraph{(3) Goal-frame convention.} The success flag fires when the
end-effector enters a $5$\,cm radius, but the scripted expert keeps refining to
$\sim$1\,cm. Taking the goal frame at first-success rather than at the expert's
final frame parked the planner just outside the success radius --- producing the
systematic ``arrives but scores zero'' pattern (final ee $2$--$4$\,cm,
success$=0$) that initially looked like a model failure.

\paragraph{Verification.} After all three fixes, rendered transitions have
one-step prediction error $1.4\times$ the dataset level and latent
nearest-neighbour ratio $0.97$ (i.e.\ on the manifold), and replaying a dataset
trajectory's raw actions in the live environment reproduces its proprioceptive
trajectory to a median end-effector error of $1.5$\,mm --- confirming that only
the visuals had drifted, never the physics. The reach baseline then recovers from
$1/16$ to $15/16$. The probe scripts that produce these numbers are released with
the code.
